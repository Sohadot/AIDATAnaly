The AIDA Transition Index

The AIDA Transition Index (ATI) is the governed standard that AIDA Transition Analytics uses to score the health of movement between funnel states. It does not score stages. It scores the four transitions that connect them.

Definition

ATI is a measurement standard with one unit of measurement: the transition. A transition is the movement of a prospect from one funnel state to the next — from being noticed to becoming interested, from being interested to wanting, from wanting to acting, and from acting to staying.

Stages are states. Value lives in transitions.

Why This Standard Exists

Most funnel reporting counts what happens inside stages: impressions, sessions, leads, orders. Those numbers describe states. They do not explain why prospects fail to move from one state to the next, how fast that movement happens, or how much of it survives friction. ATI exists to make that movement measurable, comparable over time, and diagnosable when it weakens.

The Four Transition Vectors

ATI measures exactly four vectors. Their names are fixed by the standard.

  1. Attention
  2. T1
  3. Interest
  4. T2
  5. Desire
  6. T3
  7. Action
  8. T4
  9. Loyalty

How It Works: Seven Scoring Dimensions

Each vector is scored from 0 to 100 across seven weighted dimensions.

DimensionNameWeightWhat it measures
D1Signal Quality17%Strength and meaning of the signal
D2Movement Probability23%Likelihood of transition
D3Transition Velocity11%Speed and efficiency of movement
D4Friction Resilience17%Ability to withstand obstacles
D5Drop-Off Resilience12%Resistance to abandonment
D6Commercial Consequence10%Business value of the movement
D7Intervention Clarity10%Clarity of corrective action
 
Vector Score = (D1 x 0.17) + (D2 x 0.23) + (D3 x 0.11) + (D4 x 0.17)
             + (D5 x 0.12) + (D6 x 0.10) + (D7 x 0.10)

Composite ATI = (T1 + T2 + T3 + T4) / 4

The four vectors carry equal weight by default. Any non-default weighting must be explicitly declared in the output.

Diagnostic Classes

Every scored vector falls into one of five fixed diagnostic classes.

ClassRangeMeaning
Transition Strong 85–100 Movement is healthy, clear, measurable, and commercially useful.
Transition Functional 70–84 Movement exists and is generally healthy, with visible optimization gaps.
Transition Unstable 55–69 Movement occurs inconsistently or depends on weak, fragmented signals.
Transition Constrained 40–54 Movement is weak, delayed, or structurally blocked.
Transition Critical 0–39 Movement is failing, unmeasured, or commercially ineffective.

Evidence Confidence Is Reported Separately

Every ATI output carries an Evidence Confidence level from E0 to E4. Evidence Confidence is not part of the ATI score. It qualifies how strongly the score should be interpreted, and it never changes the score mathematically.

Two consequences follow directly from the standard:

  • If a vector has E0 Absent Evidence, no numeric score is issued for that vector. It is reported as Unscorable.
  • If one or more vectors are E0, no clean composite ATI score is issued. The output is a Partial Profile instead.

What ATI Does Not Measure

  • It does not measure stage volume. Counting visitors or orders is state reporting, not transition measurement.
  • It does not measure brand sentiment or creative taste.
  • It does not predict revenue, and it does not claim exact causality.
  • It does not replace analytics platforms; it interprets movement evidence they produce.

Relationship to the Failure Ontology

When a vector scores poorly, ATI answers how weak the movement is. The Transition Failure Ontology answers why: it classifies the weakness into one of 21 governed failure modes, each with a stable ID such as tfo.t2.value_ambiguity, plus one cross-vector diagnostic constraint, tfo.constraint.measurement_gap. Recommended corrections are expressed through the ten approved intervention layers.

Relationship to the Scanner

The Transition Scanner is the public diagnostic instrument of this standard. Scanner v1 is rules-governed: it maps structured answers onto the seven dimensions, produces a T1–T4 profile, assigns Evidence Confidence per vector, and names likely failure modes using ontology terms only. The full method is described on the methodology page.

Output Examples

Correct output, fully scorable:

Composite ATI: 64 — Transition Unstable
T1: 72 / E2 Observable Evidence
T2: 46 / E1 Self-Reported Evidence (provisional)
T3: 58 / E2 Observable Evidence
T4: 80 / E3 Multi-Source Evidence

Correct output when T4 evidence is absent:

ATI Profile: Partial
Scorable vectors: T1, T2, T3
Unscorable vector: T4 / E0 Absent Evidence

Prohibited output: issuing a composite number such as ATI Score: 61.5 while any vector is E0, or hiding an unmeasured vector inside a false zero.

Public Claim Limits

AIDAtanaly introduces the AIDA Transition Index as a governed standard. It is not described as an adopted industry standard, because adoption evidence does not yet exist. ATI outputs never promise revenue outcomes, and stronger evidence levels strengthen confidence language only — they never imply guaranteed causality. The full claim policy lives on the governance page.
